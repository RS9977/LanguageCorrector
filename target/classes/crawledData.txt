<h2 id="mp-tfa-h2" class="mp-h2"><span id="From_today.27s_featured_article"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="From_today's_featured_article">From today's featured article</span></h2>
<div id="mp-tfa" class="mp-contains-float"><b><a href="/wiki/John_Littlejohn_(preacher)" title="John Littlejohn (preacher)">John Littlejohn</a></b> (1756&#8211;1836) was an American tradesman and <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodist</a> preacher. Migrating from Great Britain to the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a> when he was around 12, he traveled between various apprenticeships in Maryland and Virginia. After encountering Methodist <a href="/wiki/Revivalist_(person)" title="Revivalist (person)">revivalists</a>, Littlejohn experienced a religious awakening and began preaching as an itinerant <a href="/wiki/Circuit_rider_(religious)" title="Circuit rider (religious)">circuit rider</a> during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">Revolutionary War</a>. Harassed by colonial authorities despite his revolutionary sympathies, Littlejohn managed to evade legal persecution and settle as a local preacher and tradesman in <a href="/wiki/Leesburg,_Virginia" title="Leesburg, Virginia">Leesburg, Virginia</a>, where he variously served as a county magistrate, tax collector, and sheriff. In 1814, he was called upon in his duty as sheriff to protect the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">Declaration of Independence</a> and various other important documents evacuated during the <a href="/wiki/Burning_of_Washington" title="Burning of Washington">British raid on Washington</a>. He moved to Kentucky several years later, where he entered work as a land agent after being sold falsely advertised land. He died at his home in <a href="/wiki/Logan_County,_Kentucky" title="Logan County, Kentucky">Logan County</a> in 1836. <td style="padding:0 6px 0 0">
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/City_Palace,_Jaipur" title="City Palace, Jaipur">City Palace of Jaipur</a></b>, constructed between 1727 and 1732, is a royal residence located in <a href="/wiki/Jaipur" title="Jaipur">Jaipur</a>, Rajasthan, India. It was the ceremonial and administrative seat of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Amber" title="Kingdom of Amber">Kingdom of Amber</a>, and home of its rulers, from the time of the state's foundation in 1727 by Maharaja <a href="/wiki/Sawai_Jai_Singh" title="Sawai Jai Singh">Sawai Jai Singh</a>&#160;II until 30 March 1949. The palace continues to be a residence of the royal family of Jaipur and is a popular tourist attraction. This picture shows the <i>Leheriya</i> gate, one of four intricately designed gates in an inner courtyard of the palace. The gate is dedicated to Hindu god <a href="/wiki/Ganesha" title="Ganesha">Ganesha</a> (carved above the door) with the green colour representing the season of spring.
